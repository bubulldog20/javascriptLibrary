My journey to Web Development has been a unique path. As a college student I was focused on baseball and business, in that order. However, as reality set in I focused on a career in sales. During my eight years as a salesperson I worked in both retail and B2B environments, as both management and account rep, and in both inside and outside sales. While the experience was challenging and exciting, another path presented itself.

In my last few years in the business world I became a trainer to my customers as much as a salesperson. This led to a Transition to Teaching program and a new career as a Business Teacher and Coach. With young kids at home and a working spouse, stability was paramount. I needed steady money and to be home every night. Not to mention I was going to be getting the chance to change the world, one class of students at a time.

Still wondering how Web Development fits in? Me too, as twelve years into my teaching career I made a change from the junior high to the high school with the new task of teaching HTML and CSS. Interesting...I'm a business teacher, but I like a challenge so I focused on what I did know, web design. I didn't know the code behind it but I understood the visual appeal, usefulness, and necessity of websites in today's world. As I taught myself how to code, I began to love the challenge of figuring out the intricacies of all the parts of a website.

Fast forward two more years and I'm now a graduate of Eleven Fifty Academy. Am I an expert web developer? No. However, my background in business and education put me in the strategic position of being someone who has a specific set of skills:

  1. Speaks the language of business
  2. Educates anyone who doesn't understand the importance of websites and web design in today's economy
  3. Communicates, works with, and potentially leads a web development team on diverse projects
  4. Becomes the bridge connecting the customer and design team

Just to prove my point I am linking my résumé. You will undoubtedly notice something called Guler Company. While I grant you that I shouldn't get points for creativity, I should definitely get credit for having an entrepreneurial background considering my family has been running our own business for almost four years - while both my wife and I have been working full time.

An interviewer's job is in part to figure out if the applicant will be a good fit. I fit in everywhere, which is why I have been able to hold such a wide range of positions without ever being let go. Personable, hard-working, creative, loyal, and entrepreneurial goes a long way in the 21st century. This leaves me with one question for anyone reading this - how can I help you achieve your goals?
